SOLAR PARKER PROBE

About Mission

Launch: The Parker Solar Probe was launched on August 12, 2018, aboard a Delta IV Heavy rocket from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station in Florida. Closest Approach: The probe gets closer to the Sun than any previous spacecraft, flying through the Sun's corona, reaching distances as close as 4 million miles from the Sun's surface. Named After: Named after Dr. Eugene Parker, who proposed the existence of solar wind in the 1950s, the Parker Solar Probe aims to explore the region where solar winds are accelerated

Mission Objectives

Understand Solar Wind: Investigate the mechanisms that accelerate and transport solar wind and explore its structure and dynamics. Study Solar Corona: Examine the Sun's outer atmosphere to understand the processes that heat it and accelerate the solar wind. Explore Magnetic Fields: Investigate the magnetic fields around the Sun and how they influence solar wind and the Sun's atmosphere. Predict Space Weather: Improve our ability to forecast changes in space weather and understand their impacts on Earth and other planets.

Major Findings

Solar Wind Origin: Confirmed the existence of previously hypothesized "switchbacks" in the solar wind - sudden reversals in the magnetic field direction, providing clues about the origins of solar wind. Solar Wind Speed: Measured solar wind speeds much higher than predicted, indicating the need for further study on the mechanisms that accelerate solar wind. Coronal Heating: Observed structures in the corona that might be responsible for heating it to temperatures much higher than the solar surface. Magnetic Field Structure: Provided insights into the complex and dynamic magnetic field structures near the Sun, including the discovery of magnetic islands and switchbacks. Space Weather Impact: Contributed to our understanding of space weather and its potential impacts on technology and human activities in space.
